About Molar Pregnancies

The Hydatidiform Mole Follow-Up Service (HMFUS) is the Scottish national service for diagnosis and follow-up of hydatidiform mole (molar pregnancy) and related gestational trophoblastic disease (GTD). A molar pregnancy is a relatively uncommon pregnancy disorder, occurring in approximately 1 in 500–1,000 pregnancies. It forms part of the spectrum of gestational trophoblastic disease, which includes both benign and malignant conditions.

About the Service

We take part in diagnosis and monitoring/follow-up.

Diagnosis

If a molar pregnancy is suspected in Scotland, tissue samples are sent to the HMFUS for expert diagnosis. The laboratory performs DNA ploidy analysis on FFPE tissue using flow cytometry to support the diagnosis of molar pregnancy. Tissue sections undergo enzymatic digestion to release nuclei, which are stained with propidium iodide, a fluorescent dye that binds proportionally to DNA. Using a BD FACSCanto analyser, we measure fluorescence intensity to distinguish diploid, triploid, and other DNA content patterns. Accurate results depend on well-fixed, properly identified blocks containing sufficient chorionic villi, as low villous content may require alternative techniques such as FISH. Flow cytometry profiles are interpreted by trained staff, allowing differentiation between complete and partial molar pregnancies and guiding further testing when required.

Specialist histopathological review of tissue samples confirms or excludes molar pregnancy and related conditions.

Follow-up

If confirmed, women are enrolled in our follow-up programme, which monitors hCG levels at regular intervals using our specialised RIA system. In the UK, all women who have had a molar pregnancy are registered with a specialist centre. For those with persistent or malignant disease, chemotherapy is undertaken at Charing Cross Hospital, London.

Follow-up involves measuring the pregnancy hormone hCG in blood and urine until it returns to normal. hCG is produced by syncytiotrophoblast cells and exists in various molecular forms in malignant disease. It is expressed in nearly all malignant trophoblastic tumours (rare exceptions in PSTT). hCG levels correlate with the number of proliferating cells, guide risk assessment, and monitor treatment response.

The HMFUS uses a specialised radioimmunoassay (RIA) system for hCG monitoring and is also the national production site for iodinated hCG reagent used by all three UK GTD centres. The assay uses a monoclonal anti-hCG antibody (4F9) and ¹²⁵I-labelled hCG, incubated with patient samples overnight. Bound and free fractions are separated using a PEG-assisted double-antibody precipitation step, followed by centrifugation. The remaining radiolabelled pellet is quantified on a gamma counter, with signal intensity corresponding to total hCG concentration.
